Post Hoc Fallacy (After This, Therefore Because of This)

A logical fallacy

The post hoc fallacy, also known as "after this, therefore because of this," occurs when someone assumes that because one event follows another, the first event caused the second. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to false assumptions about causation.

For example, "I wore my lucky socks and won the game. Therefore, my lucky socks caused the win" is an example of a post hoc fallacy because it incorrectly assumes causation from mere sequence.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than false assumptions about causation. By doing so, we can avoid the post hoc fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: False Cause

Examples

Business Example: "Our sales increased after we changed our logo. Therefore, the logo change caused the sales increase."

Academic Example: "Students' grades improved after we introduced a new textbook. Therefore, the textbook caused the improvement."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use the post hoc fallacy to draw incorrect conclusions."

Intimate Partner Example: "We started arguing after you got that new job. Therefore, your new job is causing our arguments."
