Affirming the Consequent

A logical fallacy

Affirming the consequent occurs when someone incorrectly concludes that a specific condition is the cause of an outcome because the outcome is present. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to false assumptions about causation.

For example, "If it rains, the ground will be wet. The ground is wet. Therefore, it must have rained" is an example of affirming the consequent because it ignores other possible reasons for the ground being wet.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on sound reasoning and considering all possibilities, rather than false assumptions about causation. By doing so, we can avoid the affirming the consequent fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Denying the Antecedent

Examples

Business Example: "If our product is good, it will sell well. Our product is selling well. Therefore, it must be good."

Academic Example: "If the hypothesis is correct, the experiment will yield results. The experiment yielded results. Therefore, the hypothesis must be correct."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use affirming the consequent to create misunderstandings."

Intimate Partner Example: "If you love me, you will call me. You called me. Therefore, you must love me."
