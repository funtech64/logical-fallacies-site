Correlation Fallacy (Cum Hoc Fallacy)

A logical fallacy

The correlation fallacy, also known as the cum hoc fallacy, occurs when someone assumes that because two events occur together, they must be causally related. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to false assumptions about causation.

For example, "Ice cream sales increase in the summer. Shark attacks also increase in the summer. Therefore, eating ice cream causes shark attacks" is an example of a correlation fallacy because it incorrectly assumes causation from correlation.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than false assumptions about causation. By doing so, we can avoid the correlation fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Post Hoc Fallacy

Examples

Business Example: "Our sales increased after we launched our new product. Therefore, the new product caused the increase in sales."

Academic Example: "Students who use laptops in class get higher grades. Therefore, using laptops in class improves grades."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use the correlation fallacy to draw incorrect conclusions."

Intimate Partner Example: "Every time we argue, it rains. Therefore, our arguments cause the rain."
