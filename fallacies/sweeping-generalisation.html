A logical fallacy

The sweeping generalisation fallacy occurs when someone applies a general rule to a specific case to which the rule does not apply. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to overgeneralised conclusions.

For example, "Exercise is good for everyone, so it must be good for people with heart conditions" is an example of a sweeping generalisation because it ignores specific circumstances.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on specific evidence and context, rather than overgeneralised conclusions. By doing so, we can avoid the sweeping generalisation fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Hasty Generalisation

Examples

Business Example: "All successful companies use social media, so our company must use it to be successful."

Academic Example: "Reading is beneficial for all students, so it must be beneficial for students with severe dyslexia."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use sweeping generalisations to make quick judgments."

Intimate Partner Example: "All couples should spend more time together, so we should always be together."
