Subjectivist Fallacy

A logical fallacy

The subjectivist fallacy occurs when someone argues that a statement is true for one person but not for another, thereby treating subjective experiences as objective facts. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to personal beliefs and opinions.

For example, "What's true for you isn't necessarily true for me" is an example of the subjectivist fallacy because it treats personal beliefs as equivalent to objective truth.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than relying on subjective beliefs. By doing so, we can avoid the subjectivist fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Historian’s Fallacy

Examples

Business Example: "This marketing strategy works for me, but it might not work for you."

Academic Example: "The theory might be true for some researchers, but not for others."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use the subjectivist fallacy to justify their actions."

Intimate Partner Example: "You might think our relationship is fine, but I don't."
