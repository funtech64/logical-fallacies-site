Slippery Slope Fallacy

A logical fallacy

The slippery slope fallacy occurs when someone argues that a relatively small first step will inevitably lead to a chain of related events culminating in some significant (usually negative) effect. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to unfounded assumptions about future events.

For example, "If we allow students to redo assignments, next they will want to retake entire courses, and eventually, they will demand degrees without any work at all" is an example of a slippery slope fallacy because it assumes a chain reaction without evidence.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than unfounded assumptions about future events. By doing so, we can avoid the slippery slope fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: False Cause

Examples

Business Example: "If we allow employees to work from home, soon everyone will want to work from home, and then no work will get done."

Academic Example: "If we start grading more leniently, students will stop trying, and eventually, academic standards will plummet."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use the slippery slope fallacy to dramatize potential consequences."

Intimate Partner Example: "If we start arguing about small things, it will escalate, and eventually, our relationship will fall apart."
