Argument from Authority

A logical fallacy

An argument from authority relies on the opinion of an authority figure instead of evidence and logical reasoning. This fallacy shifts the focus from the argument's merits to the credibility of the authority.

For example, "This medicine must be effective because a famous doctor endorses it" is an argument from authority because it relies on the doctor's authority rather than scientific evidence.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on their logical and evidential merits, regardless of who presents them. By doing so, we can avoid the argument from authority fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Appeal to Authority

Examples

Business Example: "Our product is the best because a well-known CEO uses it."

Academic Example: "This theory must be true because a famous professor supports it."

Pop-Culture Example: "In advertisements, products are often endorsed by celebrities to persuade people."

Intimate Partner Example: "This relationship advice must be good because a relationship expert gave it."
