Amphiboly / Ambiguity

A logical fallacy

Amphiboly, or ambiguity, occurs when a statement or argument is unclear due to vague or ambiguous language. This fallacy shifts the focus from the actual content of the argument to its ambiguous presentation.

For example, "Flying planes can be dangerous" can mean either that the act of flying planes is dangerous or that planes themselves are dangerous when flying.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on their logical and evidential merits, rather than their ambiguous presentation. By doing so, we can avoid amphiboly and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Accent Fallacies

Examples

Business Example: "We need to discuss the project with our team members who are available."

Academic Example: "The professor said on Monday he would give a lecture."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use amphiboly to create humorous misunderstandings."

Intimate Partner Example: "I saw her duck."
