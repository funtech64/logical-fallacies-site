Fallacy of Aggregate

A logical fallacy

The fallacy of aggregate occurs when someone assumes that what is true for individual members of a group is also true for the group as a whole. This fallacy shifts the focus from the aggregate to the individual members without justification.

For example, "If every student in the class is above average, then the class must be above average" is a fallacy of aggregate because it assumes the properties of individual members apply to the group as a whole without evidence.

Critical thinking involves evaluating the properties of the group based on evidence, rather than assuming they are the same as the properties of individual members. By doing so, we can avoid the fallacy of aggregate and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Composition Fallacy

Examples

Business Example: "Each employee is highly skilled, so the company must be highly skilled."

Academic Example: "Each experiment produced good results, so the research as a whole must be good."

Pop-Culture Example: "In movies, characters often assume that if each part of a plan is good, the entire plan must be good."

Intimate Partner Example: "Each date we've had has been wonderful, so our relationship must be wonderful."
