Suppressed Evidence

A logical fallacy

The suppressed evidence fallacy occurs when someone intentionally omits relevant and significant information in an argument. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to selective presentation of evidence.

For example, "The new drug was tested and found to be effective" is an example of suppressed evidence if the speaker fails to mention significant side effects that were also discovered.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on all available evidence and logical reasoning, rather than selectively presented information. By doing so, we can avoid the suppressed evidence fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Appeal to Ignorance

Examples

Business Example: "Our product has been proven to improve health" (omitting that it only improves a specific aspect of health).

Academic Example: "This study supports my theory" (omitting that other studies contradict it).

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use suppressed evidence to convince others."

Intimate Partner Example: "You always forget our anniversaries" (omitting that the partner remembered several other important dates).
