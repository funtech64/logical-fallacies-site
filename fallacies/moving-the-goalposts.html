Moving the Goalposts

A logical fallacy

Moving the goalposts occurs when the criteria for proof or acceptance of an argument are changed after they have been met. This fallacy shifts the focus from the original standards to new, often unattainable ones.

For example, "You met the sales target this month, but to really prove your ability, you need to meet next month's target too" is moving the goalposts because it changes the criteria for success after the initial target was met.

Critical thinking involves holding to consistent standards and criteria. By doing so, we can avoid the moving the goalposts fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Special Pleading

Examples

Business Example: "You've met your sales quota for this quarter, but now you need to exceed it to prove your worth."

Academic Example: "You've completed your thesis, but to graduate with honors, you need to publish your research."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often change the criteria for success to keep others from succeeding."

Intimate Partner Example: "You've apologized for missing our anniversary, but to make up for it, you need to plan a special event."
