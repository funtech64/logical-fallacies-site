Appeal to Consequences

A logical fallacy

An appeal to consequences occurs when someone argues that a belief or action is correct because it leads to desirable or undesirable consequences. This fallacy shifts the focus from truth to consequences.

For example, "If we don't believe in free will, society will fall into chaos" is an appeal to consequences because it relies on the consequences of the belief rather than its truth.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than consequences alone. By doing so, we can avoid the appeal to consequences fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Appeal to Pragmatism

Examples

Business Example: "We must adopt this strategy because it will increase profits, regardless of its ethical implications."

Academic Example: "We should support this theory because it leads to positive outcomes."

Pop-Culture Example: "In movies, characters often use consequences to justify their actions."

Intimate Partner Example: "We should do this because it will make us happier, even if it's not fair."
