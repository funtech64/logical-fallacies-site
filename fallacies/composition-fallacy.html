Composition Fallacy

A logical fallacy

The composition fallacy occurs when someone assumes that what is true for the parts is also true for the whole. This fallacy shifts the focus from valid reasoning to incorrect assumptions about wholes and parts.

For example, "Each member of the team is highly skilled, so the team must be highly skilled" is a composition fallacy because it assumes that the team's overall skill is equivalent to the sum of its parts.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on logical reasoning and evidence, rather than incorrect assumptions about wholes and parts. By doing so, we can avoid the composition fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Division Fallacy

Examples

Business Example: "Our product uses the best materials, so it must be the best product."

Academic Example: "Each chapter of the book is well-written, so the book must be well-written."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use composition fallacies to make assumptions."

Intimate Partner Example: "Each aspect of our relationship is good, so our relationship must be perfect."
