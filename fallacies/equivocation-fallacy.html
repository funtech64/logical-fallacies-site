Equivocation Fallacy

A logical fallacy

An equivocation fallacy occurs when someone uses a word with multiple meanings in different senses within the same argument, leading to confusion and ambiguity. This fallacy shifts the focus from clear reasoning to equivocation.

For example, "A feather is light, and what is light cannot be dark, so a feather cannot be dark" is an equivocation fallacy because it relies on different meanings of the word "light."

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and clear reasoning, rather than equivocation. By doing so, we can avoid the equivocation fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Amphiboly

Examples

Business Example: "Our product is light, and light products are superior, so our product is superior."

Academic Example: "This word has multiple meanings, so both interpretations must be correct."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use equivocation to create humor or confusion."

Intimate Partner Example: "You said you wanted to be happy, but now you're upset."
