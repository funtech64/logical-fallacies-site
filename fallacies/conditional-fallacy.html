Conditional Fallacy

A logical fallacy

The conditional fallacy occurs when someone assumes a conditional statement is true in all cases without considering exceptions. This fallacy shifts the focus from sound reasoning to unfounded assumptions about conditional relationships.

For example, "If it is raining, then the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore, it must be raining" is an example of a conditional fallacy because it ignores other possible reasons for the ground being wet.

Critical thinking involves evaluating arguments based on evidence and logical reasoning, rather than unfounded assumptions about conditional relationships. By doing so, we can avoid the conditional fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: False Cause

Examples

Business Example: "If we increase our advertising, sales will increase. Sales have increased. Therefore, it must be because of our advertising."

Academic Example: "If a student studies hard, they will get good grades. This student got good grades. Therefore, they must have studied hard."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often use the conditional fallacy to draw incorrect conclusions."

Intimate Partner Example: "If you love me, you will call me. You called me. Therefore, you must love me."
