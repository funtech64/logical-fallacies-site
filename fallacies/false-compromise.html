False Compromise / Middle Ground Fallacy

A logical fallacy

The false compromise or middle ground fallacy occurs when someone assumes that the middle position between two extremes must be correct simply because it is the middle position. This fallacy shifts the focus from logical reasoning to a false sense of balance.

For example, "If one person says the sky is blue and another says it's green, then it must be some shade of blue-green" is a false compromise because it assumes the middle ground is correct without evidence.

Critical thinking involves evaluating the merits of each position based on evidence and reasoning, rather than assuming the middle ground is always correct. By doing so, we can avoid the false compromise fallacy and maintain a focus on the issues at hand.

See also: Appeal to Moderation

Examples

Business Example: "If one department wants a budget increase and another wants a decrease, we should keep the budget the same."

Academic Example: "If one study shows a positive effect and another shows no effect, the true effect must be somewhere in between."

Pop-Culture Example: "In TV shows, characters often compromise between two extreme positions to avoid conflict."

Intimate Partner Example: "If one partner wants to go out and the other wants to stay in, they decide to go out for a short while."
